Computer System

  • A computer is an electronic device which takes input from the user through different input devices, processes it & finally gives output to the user through different output devices.

  • The term Computer is derived from the Latin word “Computare” which means to calculate.

Characteristics of Computer

The major characteristics of computer are below:

  1. Speed

    • The computer is a very high speed electronic device. It can perform several calculations much faster than humans. It can perform millions, billions of operations in data within a second. The time used by computers to perform an operation is called processing speed.

  2. Accuracy

    • In addition to being very fast, a computer is a very accurate device. The term accuracy simply refers to the out of providing measure without any error. The output of a computer totally depends on the given instruction & input data.
    • If input data is incorrect then resultant output will be incorrect. In computer terminology GIGO (Garbage-In Garbage-Out) condition.

  3. Automation

    • Computers can automatically perform operations without intervention of the user during the operation. It automatically controls different devices attached with the computer & executes the program instruction one by one.

  4. Versatility

    • A computer is a versatile machine due to its ability to perform a wide range of tasks across various domains. The same computer can be used for various things such as accounting, generating bills, playing games, and listening music, etc.

  5. Storage

    • A computer can store large amounts of data in its memory. The stored data can be retrieved and processed whenever required.

  6. Diligence

    • Unlike human beings, a computer never feels tired or bored to do a task repeating for a long time. The computer can perform the same speed task with same speed & accuracy even for hours & days so long as it is provided with a suitable environment & correct program.

  7. Non-Intelligence

    • Although computers are incredibly, more powerful & accurate than humans, they don't have common sense. It can't think of deciding itself to do any task.

History of Computer

  • The development of computers was not spontaneous. Many scientists and many activities contributed to the history of computers.

  • The historical time period can be divided into different eras:

    1. Pre-mechanical Era

      • This was the time period between 500BC to 1642 AD. The details of the different devices invented during this era are given below:

        1. Abacus

          • The Abacus is the earliest and the simplest calculating device which is also known as "Soroban". It was developed and used in China about 500BC(Before Christ).
          • It consists of a rectangular wooden frame having parallel wires(string) and each wire contains a number of beads.
          • It was used for simple addition and subtraction.

        2. Calculating device of John Napier

          • In the early 17th century, the famous logarithm idea was developed by John Napier (Scottish Mathematician).
          • He created a logarithm table for arithmetic calculations. He also developed a new manual calculating device known as Napier's Bone.
          • It is used for Multiplication and Division.

        3. Slide Rule

          • The commonly used calculating device known as "Slide Rules" was also developed using the concept of Napier's idea of logarithms.
          • Slide rule was first built in England in 1632. With the modern slide rule we can perform simple arithmetic calculations as well as calculate square roots, logs, sine, cosine and tangents, etc.

    2. Mechanical Era

      • The mechanical era started from 1642 AD to 1843 AD. The details of the different devices invented during this era are given below:

        1. Calculating Device of Blaise Pascal (1642-1662)

          • In 1642 famous mathematician Blaise Pascal of France invented the mechanical calculating device or machine called Pascaline.
          • It is used to perform arithmetic calculations like addition, subtraction, multiplication and division and display the numbers by rotation of different wheels.
          • Pascal's calculating machine could perform the addition and subtraction operations directly but multiplication and division operations were performed by repeated addition and subtraction.

        2. Calculating Device of Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

          • In 1671 German mathematician Gottfried developed first calculating for multiplication as well as for other simple arithmetic calculations.
          • It was the modified form of Pascal's Calculating ing machine and it was more reliable and accurate.

        3. Jacquard Loom

          • Joseph Marie, an engineer of French, invented the machine called Jacquard loom (1801) used in weaving.
          • It was a programmable manufacturing device. It was a machine that used punched cards to store information.
          • This machine can store information which helps in computer evolution and the textile industry.

        4. Era of Charles Babbage

          • In 1822, the English mathematician Charles Babbage at Cambridge University, UK, proposed a steam-driven calculating machine called the Difference Engine, which was the size of a room.
          • The Difference Engine was designed to compute and print mathematical tables, such as logarithmic and trigonometric tables, which were essential for navigation, engineering, and science.
          • In 1833, after about 10 years, Babbage designed another machine called the Analytical Engine. This device was very large and powered by steam.
          • The Analytical Engine had several key features, including the ability to accept input data, store information, and perform arithmetic calculations.
          • Although Babbage never completed the Analytical Engine, his design laid the foundation for modern computers, incorporating essential concepts such as input, output, storage, and processing. For this reason, Babbage is often called the “Father of the Computer.”

        5. Lady Augusta Ada (First Lady Programmer)

          • A Lady student Augusta Ada Byron, When she was 19, heard a lecture about the difference and Analytical engine designed by Babbage and was fascinated by Babbage's idea.
          • Through several meetings and letters with the babbage, she learned about the design of Babbage's engine and started to write programs for the still incomplete machine.
          • Ada wrote a series of “Notes” and subroutines(set of instructions) for the Analytical Engine.

        6. George Boole

          • George Boole is a 19th century mathematician who created Boolean algebra, which uses true(1) or false(0) values to solve problems.
          • His work is crucial for modern computers and digital technology. Boole's ideas help make computers work the way they do today.

    3. Electromechanical Era

      • The end of the mechanical era occurred when physics paved the way for electrical innovation. The beads of the Abacus were replaced by bits in the modern computer. The creation of bit marked a transition from the decimal binary system for systems for humans to a binary computer.

      • The different devices invented during this era are given below.

        1. Punched Card Tabulating Machine

          • In 1890, an American scientist Herman Hollerith used the idea of a punched board system and introduced the punched cards as input metal in computers.
          • They developed the first electro mechanical punched card tabulator. This machine could read information that had been punched into cards.

        2. Atanasoff-Berry Computer (ABC)

          • In 1937-1942, Dr. John Atanasoff, a professor of law at state university and his assistant Clifford Berry designed an electronic machine which was called Atarasaff-Berry Computer.
          • It was the first to use binary representation of data and electronic Switching elements.
          • The ABC was designed to solve linear algebra problems and helps to derive principles for modern computing, such as binary arithmetic and electronic logic circuits.

        3. Mark-I or ASCC

          • In 1944 an American professor of Harvard University Dr. Howard Aiken designed the first fully automatic calculating machine which was named Mark-1.
          • It is also known as ASCC (Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator).
          • This machine operated under the control of instructions which were given through the punched paper tape.

    4. Electronic Era

      • The electronic Era, marked by rapid advancement in technology, computer communication, entertainment, and information access.
      • Innovations like the internet, smartphones, and digital media have to transform daily life, business operations and social interactions.

      • The different devices invented during this era are:

        1. ENIAC (1943-1946)

          • ENIAC stands for “Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer”.
          • It was the first electronic computer.
          • It was developed in 1943 by J.P. Eckert and John Mauchly at Moore School of Electrical Engineering in the U.S.A.
          • It contained about 1800 vacuum tubes and occupied more than 1500 sq. feet with a weight of 30 tons.

        2. EDVAC (1946-1952)

          • The EDVAC (Electronic Discrete Variable Automatic Computer) was one of the earliest electronic computers and marked a significant advancement in the field of computing.
          • It was programmed by physically connecting wires in proper order which was very difficult.
          • This problem was overcome by a new concept of stored program (store both instruction and data in the binary form) presented by John Von Neumann.

        3. EDSAC (1947-1949)

          • EDSAC stands for "Electronic Delayed Storage Automatic Computer".
          • It was developed in 1948 at Cambridge University by scientists headed by groups of Professor Maurice Wilkes.
          • This device was used for computational biology, mathematics, physics and engineering.

        4. UNIVAC (1951)

          • UNIVAC stands for Universal Automatic Computer. It was also developed by J.P Eckert and John Mauchly (designer of ENIAC) in 1951.
          • It was the first digital computer and the input was fed into this computer through magnetic tape.
          • The was one of the first commercially produced computers in the United States.
          • It was notable for its uses in business and government app applications, such as the US census, and predicting the outcomes of the 1952 presidential election.

Generation of Computer

  • The term “Generation of Computers” refers to the evolution of computer technology over distinct time periods, each marked by significant changes in hardware, software, system architecture, and programming languages.

    1. First Generation (1940-1950)

      • These Computers were the earliest electronic computers and represented significant advancement in technology. Here are some Characteristics of first-generation of Computers:
      • Technology: Vacuum tubes.
      • Input/Output: Punch cards for input; printed paper for output.
      • Storage: Magnetic drum memory.
      • Programming: Machine language (0 & 1), no operating system.

      • Examples: ENIAC, UNIVAC-I.

        Disadvantages

        1. Very large in size, consuming high power.
        2. Limited storage (few KB).
        3. High cost and low reliability.
        4. Generated excessive heat.

    2. Second Generation Computer (1950-1960)

      • The key features of this generation are following:
      • Technology: Transistors replaced vacuum tubes, smaller and more efficient.
      • Input/Output: Punch cards, magnetic tape; printers for output.
      • Storage: Magnetic core memory (primary), magnetic tape (secondary).
      • Programming: High-level languages introduced (FORTRAN, COBOL).

      • Examples:IBM 1401, IBM 7030, IBM 7090, etc.

        Disadvantages

        1. Still large and relatively expensive.
        2. Limited graphics capability.
        3. Transistors generated heat, requiring cooling systems.

    3. Third Generation Computer (1960-1970)

      • The key features of this generation are as follows.
      • Technology: Integrated Circuits (ICs) replaced individual transistors.
      • Input/Output: Keyboards, punch cards; monitors (VDUs) and printers.
      • Storage: Magnetic tapes, disk drives, early removable media (floppy disks).
      • Advantages: Faster and smaller than second-generation computers.

      • Examples: IBM 360 Series, PDP-8, Honeywell 600 series, etc.

        Disadvantages

        1. High cost and complex design.
        2. Overheating is possible due to compact arrangement of components.

    4. Fourth Generation (1970 - Present)

      • Technology: Microprocessors with LSI (Large-Scale Integration) technology.
      • Input/Output: Keyboard, mouse, joystick; monitors, printers, speakers.
      • Storage: Floppy disks, hard disk drives, CD-ROMs.
      • Advantages: Smaller, faster, more affordable, and widely accessible.

      • Examples: IBM PCs, Intel PCs, Macintosh Plus.

        Disadvantages

        1. Manufacturing microprocessor chips requires sophisticated technology.

    5. Fifth Generation (Present - Future)

      • The generation is characterized by advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), parallel processing. Key features of fifth-Generation computers are.
      • Artificial Intelligence (AI): Machine learning, neural networks, expert systems.
      • Parallel Processing:Multiple computations performed simultaneously.
      • Multi-Core Processors:Multiple processing units on a single chip.
      • Natural Language Processing (NLP): Machines understand and interact with human language.
      • Emerging Technologies:5G, IoT, bio-chips, virtual reality.

Classification of Computer on the basis of size and Speed

  • The classification of computers based on speed and size refers to grouping computers according to their physical dimensions (size) and their processing capabilities (speed).

  • On the basis of size and processing speed; computer can be classified into following types:

    1. SuperComputer

      • A supercomputer is a highly advanced computing Machine designed to perform complex & large-scale computations at exceptionally high speed.
      • Processing Speed:- Capable of performing trillions to quadrillions of calculations per Second and speed is measured in FLOPS (Floating Point Operations Per Second).
      • Parallel Processing:- Uses a technique called Parallel processing, where multiple work on different parts of a problem simultaneously. It contains tens of thousands of processors.
      • Massive storage and Memory:- Require enormous amounts of memory to handle the vast amounts of data involved in their calculations. The memory can be in the range of terabytes(TB) or even petabytes(PB).
      • Applications:- Scientific Research, Engineering, Medicine, AI etc.
      • Examples:- Fugaku, Summit, PARAM etc.

    2. Mainframe Computer

      • A mainframe computer is a large, powerful, and highly reliable computing system designed to handle and process vast amounts of data quickly. It is primarily used by large organizations for bulk data processing such as static, census data processing, transaction processing etc.
      • High Processing Power: Capable of processing billions of instructions per second (BIPS).
      • Reliability: Built with redundancy and failover features for continuous operation.
      • Security: Advanced encryption and access control for sensitive data.
      • Multi-user Capability: Supports thousands of users simultaneously without performance degradation.
      • Applications: Banking, finance, healthcare, government data processing.
      • Examples: IBM z series, System z9, System z10.

    3. Mini Computer

      • Minicomputers are mid-range computers that emerged in the 1980s. They are smaller and less expensive than mainframes but more powerful than microcomputers.
      • Size and Cost: Smaller than mainframes, often fitting in a single cabinet, and more affordable.
      • Multi-user Capability: Supports multiple users via terminals.
      • Customizable: Can connect to various peripherals like disk drives, tape drives, printers, and networks.
      • Examples:- PDP-8, PDP-11, IBM system 3, etc.

    4. Microcomputer

      • A microcomputer is a small, relatively inexpensive computer that uses a microprocessor as its CPU. Designed for individual users, it forms the foundation of personal computing, including modern PCs, laptops, tablets, and smartphones.
      • Microprocessor-Based: The CPU is integrated into a single chip.
      • Compact Size: Small enough to fit on a desk (desktop) or be portable (laptops).
      • User-Friendly: Designed for operation without specialized knowledge.
      • Storage and Peripherals: Early microcomputers used floppy disks or cassettes; modern ones use hard drives, CDs, DVDs, or SSDs.
      • Single-User Operation: Primarily intended for one user at a time.
      • Examples:- IBM PC, Apple Macintosh, Deller.

Computer architecture

  • Computer architecture refers to those attributes of a system visible to a programmer or those attributes that have a direct impact on the logical execution of a program.
  • It includes a series of instructions, addressing modes, data types and registers.

Computer organization

  • Computer organization concerns the operational units and their interconnections. It consists of physical units like circuit design and peripherals.

Components of computer system

  • A computer system is an integrated set of hardware and software designed to perform computational tasks.

  • It includes the central processing units (CPU), memory storage, input/output, devices and the operating system, which work together to execute programs and process data.

  1. Input unit

    • Input units are those units that are used for providing data and instruction to the computer. The input can be provided either by clicking on the mouse or by typing on the keyboard.

  2. CPU

    • It is the major component of the computer system. It acts as the heart and brain of a computer and performs all the processing related tasks. The CPU is the combination of three components ALU, CU and Register.

      1. ALU:- All the arithmetic and logical calculations are performed by ALU. Arithmetic operations include addition, subtraction, division and multiplication. Whereas logical units perform logical operations like AND, OR, NOT, etc.
      2. CU:- It controls all the other units in the computer by providing the control signal. The control unit fetches the instruction from memory, decodes it and finally executes the instructions.
      3. Register:-Register is a special temporary storage location within the CPU. To execute an instruction the control of the CPU retrieves it from main memory and places it onto a register.

  3. Memory

    • Memory in a computer system refers to the handware component where data and programs entered through the input unit are stored. Its main function is to store data, information or final results before display or sent to output devices.

    • Memory can be divided into two types:

      1. Primary memory

        • The primary memory is also called the main memory of the computer. It stores and provides information very quickly. This memory is generally used to hold the program being currently executed in the computer.
        • RAM and ROM are examples of the main memory.

      2. Secondary memory

        • The secondary memory is also called Auxiliary memory and has very large storage capacity. This memory is used as a backup memory for future reference. It stores several programs, documents, databases, etc.
        • The programs that you run on a computer are first transferred to the primary memory before it runs. Whenever the results are saved again they get a Hard disk, stored in the secondary memory. Floppy disk, SSD, etc. are the examples.

  4. Output Units

    • The unit in a computer system refers to the different hardware components that provide information and results of computation to the outside world in the form that con perceive, such as text, images, sounds, etc.
    • Examples: Monitor, Printers, Speakers, etc.

Concept of Microprocessor

  • The CPU contains three components ALU, CU and Register. When all these three components are integrated into a single chip then it is called Microprocessor.

Functions of Microprocessor

  1. It controls the input and output devices of the computer.
  2. It controls the storage of data inside a computer.
  3. It performs arithmetical and logical operations.
  4. It handles every other part of the computer.

Characteristics of Microprocessor

  1. Instruction set:- The set of instructions that the microprocessor can execute.
  2. Bandwidth:- The number of bits processed in a single instruction.
  3. Clock speed:- The clock speed determines how many instructions per second the processor can execute. It measures in Megahertz (MHz) or Gigahertz (GHz).
  4. Word Length:- It refers to the number of internal structures. The word length ranges from 4 bits to 64 bits depending upon the type of microprocessor.

Type of Microprocessor

  1. CISC (Complex instruction Set Computer)

    • It is an architecture that uses a large and complex set of instructions to perform a task. It has a large instruction set that leads to slower execution times. CISC processors were used in early personal computers and mainframes.

  2. RISC (Reduced instruction Set Computer)

    • It is a type of microprocessor architecture that uses a smaller, simpler set of processing sp instructions compared to complex instruction set computers (CISC). This simplicity leads to faster processing speeds, lower power consumption, and smaller chip size. RISC processors are widely used in modern devices like smartphones, tablets, and embedded systems.

Bus Structure

  • Bus structure simply refers to the pathway through which data and information can be exchanged between the different components of the computer System.
  • It consists of the data bus, address bus and control bus, each serving different roles.

Type of Computer Bus

  1. Data Bus:- This bus is bidirectional. It allows data to be transferred both to and from the CPU, memory, and I/O devices.
  2. Address Bus:- This bus is unidirectional. The address bus carries information about the location of data in memory.
  3. Control Bus:- The control bus carries the control signals that makes sure everything is flowing smoothly from place to place. It is also bidirectional.

Major function of System Bus

  1. Data transfer:- Carry data between different components of a computer system.
  2. Addressing:- Determine the location in memory or device where data should be read from or written to.
  3. Control signal transmission:- Carry control signals from the processor to other components to manage operation.
  4. Power Distribution:- Distribute electrical power to various components within a System.

Memory

  • In computers, memory is a device that stores data and instructions for a computer system. It's crucial for the computer to function, as it holds everything from the operating system to the data being processed.

  • On the basis of levels it's divided into following categories:-

    1. Register

      • Registers are small, fast storage locations within the CPU that temporarily hold data and instructions being wed by the processor.
      • They facilitate rapid data access and manipulation, enabling efficient execution of operations.
      • Examples include the accumulator, instruction register, and program counter.

    2. Cache Memory

      • Cache memory is high-speed memory used to speed up CPU execution.
      • It is more expensive than RAM but cheaper than registers and acts as a buffer between the CPU and RAM.
      • It stores frequently used data and instructions so the CPU can access them immediately.

      • Types of cache:

        1. L1 cache:- faster, smaller Cache directly on the CPU for immediate data access.
        2. L2 cache:- Larger, slightly slower cache near the CPU for frequently accessed data.
        3. L3 cache:- Largest, slowest cache Shared across CPU cores for less frequently owned data.

    3. Primary Memory

      • Primary memory, also called main memory, stores and provides information very quickly.

      • It is used to hold programs and data that are currently being executed by the computer.

        1. RAM (RANDOM ACCESS MEMORY)

          • RAM is a type of volatile memory used by computers to store data that is actively being accessed or processed.
          • It allows the CPU to quickly read and write data, enabling efficient multitasking and smooth application performance.
          • Unlike permanent storage, RAM loses all stored information when the computer is powered off.
          • Increasing RAM can improve system performance by allowing more applications to run simultaneously without slowing down.

          • It is simply divided into two types:

            1. SRAM (Static Random Access Memory)
              • SRAM is a type of memory that uses latching circuitry (flip-flops) to store each bit, making it faster and more reliable than DRAM.
              • SRAM is commonly used in cache memory and other applications where high speed is critical.
              • However, SRAM is more expensive and occupies more space than DRAM, limiting its use to specific high-performance areas.
            2. DRAM (Dynamic Random Access Memory)
              • DRAM stores each bit of data using a capacitor-transistor pair, making it more space-efficient and cost-effective than SRAM.
              • Because the capacitor leaks charge, the data must be refreshed periodically.
              • DRAM is slower than SRAM but is widely used as the main memory in computers due to its high density and lower cost.
              • Its simple design allows large amounts of memory to be packed into a small space.
              Differences Between SRAM and DRAM
              SRAM DRAM
              1. Mostly it is formed by a wing transistor. 1. Mostly it is formed by using capacitors.
              2. It is expensive. 2. It is less expensive.
              3. It has a low density of data. 3. It has a high density of data.
              4. It is faster than DRAM. 4. It is slower than SRAM.
              5. It does not need periodic refreshing. 5. It needs periodic refreshing.
              6. It is mainly used as cache memory. 6. It is used as the main memory.
              7. It consumes less electrical power. 7. It consumes high electrical power.

        2. ROM

          • ROM is a type of non-volatile memory, meaning it retains data even when the power is turned off. This makes it essential for storing firmware and other critical software.
          • ROM is usually programmed during the manufacturing process and contains data that does not need to be modified, such as the BIOS in computers.
          • Unlike RAM, traditional ROM can only be written once, though variations like EEPROM allow limited rewriting.
          • It is commonly used in devices such as computers, smartphones, and gaming consoles to store software that must not change, like boot-loaders.

          • Types of ROM:-

            1. Mask ROM:-
              • In Mask ROM, Data is written during the manufacturing process and cannot be altered or reprogrammed. Used in devices where firmware is fixed and does not require updates.
            2. PROM
              • PROM stands for Programmable ROM.
              • Manufactured as blank and can be programmed only once using a special device called a programmer. Suitable for applications where data is written after manufacturing and does not need changes.
            3. EPROM
              • EPROM stands for Erasable Programmable ROM.
              • Data can be erased by exposing the chip to ultraviolet light and then reprogrammed. Used when firmware updates are required occasionally.
            4. EEPROM
              • EEPROM stands for Electrically Erasable Programmable ROM.
              • Data can be erased and reprogrammed electrically multiple times. Commonly used in microcontrollers and BIOS where periodic updates are needed.

    4. Secondary Memory

      • Secondary memory, also known as auxiliary or external memory, is a type of computer memory used for long-term storage of data and programs.
      • Unlike primary memory (RAM), which is volatile and used for temporary storage, secondary memory is non-volatile, meaning it remains data even when the computer is turned off.

      • Some examples are:

        1. Hard Disk Drives (HDDs)

          • Technology: HDDs use magnetic storage to read and write data on spinning disks called platters. A read/write head moves across the platter to access data.
          • Capacity: Offer large storage capacities, ranging from hundreds of GB to several TB.
          • Speed: Slower read/write speeds compared to SSDs.
          • Durability: Prone to mechanical failure due to moving parts.
          • Usage: Desktop computers, laptops, and servers.

        2. Solid-State Drives (SSDs)

          • Technology: SSDs use NAND flash memory, a non-volatile memory that retains data without power.
          • Capacity: Typically range from 128 GB to several terabytes.
          • Speed: Much faster than HDDs, with speeds from about 500 MB/s to over 3,000 MB/s.
          • Durability: More durable than HDDs as they have no moving parts.
          • Usage: Modern laptops and desktops.

        3. Optical Disks

          • Technology: Use laser technology to read and write data by creating tiny pits on the disc surface.
          • Types: CDs, DVDs, and Blu-rays.
          • Capacity: CDs hold ~700 MB, DVDs up to 4.7 GB (single-layer) or 8.5 GB (dual-layer), and Blu-rays up to 25 GB or 50 GB.
          • Durability: Scratches on the data surface can cause data loss.
          • Usage: Media storage, software distribution, and backups.

        4. Flash Memory

          • Technology: Uses NAND flash memory similar to SSDs.
          • Types: USB flash drives and memory cards (SD, microSD).
          • Capacity: Ranges from a few gigabytes to several terabytes.
          • Durability: Portable and durable with no moving parts.
          • Usage: Smartphones, cameras, tablets, etc.

        5. Magnetic Tapes

          • Technology: Uses magnetic tape with sequential data access, resulting in slower access times.
          • Capacity: Stores very large amounts of data, often several terabytes.
          • Durability: Reliable for long-term storage.
          • Usage: Backups and archival storage.

Input Devices

  • An input device is hardware that allows users to enter data and control signals into a computer. For eg: keyboards, mouse, scanners, cameras, joysticks, and microphones.
  • These devices enable interaction with the computer, facilitating various tasks and operations. Input devices are essential for user-computer communication. They convert user actions into data the computer can process.

  • The different input devices are following:

    1. Keyboard

      • A keyboard is an input device used to enter text, numbers, and other commands into a computer.
      • It consists of a set of keys, each of which corresponds to a specific character, symbol, function. Keyboards are essential for typing documents, coding, gaming, and general tasks.
      • A normal keyboard contains 104 keys while a laptop contains 78-84 keys depending upon model.
      • QWERTY, Mechanical, Gaming, etc. are the examples of keyboards.

    2. Mouse

      • A mouse is a pointing device used to interact with the graphical user interface (GUI) of a computer by moving a pointer or cursor on the screen.
      • The mouse has two or three buttons on top of it.
      • For eg: Optical mouse, Mechanical mouse.

    3. Touch Screen

      • A Touch Screen is a display that also serves as an input device, allowing users to interact with the computer by touching the screen directly.
      • Examples: Smart phones, tablets, and touchscreen laptops. Users can tap, swipe or pinch the screen to navigate through menus, enter information or interact with applications.

    4. Microphone

      • An input device that captures sound and converts it into electrical signals that the computer can process.
      • A microphone used for voice commands, recording or video conferencing.

Output Devices

  • The output unit in a computer system refers to different hardware components that provide information and results of computation to the outside world in the form that can perceive such as text, images, audios, videos etc.

  • Types of output devices are:

    1. Soft-copy Output devices

      • Soft-copy devices refer to devices that display or prevent data electronically, rather than producing a physical copy.

      • Common examples include: Monitors, Projectors, Speakers.

        1. Monitor

          • A monitor is the soft copy output device which presents data and information to the user in the form of text, images, and videos.
          • It is one of the most common output devices. It is also called a display screen.
          • It displays everything from the operating system's graphical user interface (GUI) to videos and games.

          • CRT, LCD and LED are the types of monitor.

            1. CRT (Cathode Ray Tube) monitors
              • CRT monitors were the earliest type of computer monitors, using election beams to illuminate phosphor dots on the screen, which creates the images.
              • Bulk and heavy.
              • Good colour reproduction and viewing angles.
              • High power consumption.
            2. LCD (Liquid Crystal Display) monitors
              • LCD monitors are flat-panes displays that use liquid crystal to form images. The crystals are illuminated by a backlight.
              • Thin and lightweight.
              • Low power consumption.
              • Better display resolution and sharpness compared to CRTs.
            3. LED (Light Emitting Diode) monitors
              • LED monitors are a sub-type of LCD monitors where the back light is provided by LEDs (Light Emitting Diode) instead of traditional fluorescent light.
              • Thinner and more energy-efficient than standard LCDs.
              • Enhanced contrast and colour accuracy.
              • Longer Life span and lower power consumption compared to LCDs.

        2. Speaker

          • Speakers are also soft copy output devices that convert audio signals from the computer. It allows users to hear music and other sounds.
          • In most of the computer devices speakers are built-in like laptops, mobile devices, etc.
          • They are used to play music, movie audio or system sound.

        3. Projector

          • Projector is a soft copy output device that projects Images or videos from a computer onto a Large screen, making it ideal for presentations or media playback.
          • It is used in classrooms, conference rooms, or home theaters.
          • These projectors are connected to a computer to display slides, videos, or other content on a wall or screen.

    2. Hard-copy Output devices

      • Hardcopy output devices produce a physical, permanent copy of data.

      • Examples include printers, plotters, fax machines.

        1. Printer

          • A printer produces a hard copy of digital documents by converting electronic data such as text, images into a printed form.

          • Printers are usually divided into two types:

            1. Impact Printers
              • An impact printer has mechanisms resembling those of a typewriter. It forms the characters or image by striking a mechanism such as a print hammer or wheel agate on a linked ribbon, leaving an image on paper.

              • Types of Impact Printers are:

                1. Dot matrix printer

            2. Non-Impact Printers
              • Non impact printers used almost everywhere now, are faster and quality than impact printers because they have fewer moving parts.
              • It forms characters and images without direct physical contact between the printing mechanism and the paper.

              • Type of non-impact printer are:

                1. Inkjet printer
                  • Inkjet printers are non-impact pointers which print text and images by spraying tiny droplets of liquid ink onto the paper; most inkjet manufactures use this technology in consumer inkjet printers.
                2. Laser printers

Hardware Interfaces

  • It is the part through which data is transferred between a computer and other devices connected to the computer.
  • In simple words, we can say that hardware interfaces are ports, plugs or sockets through which information flows between the computer and peripherals.

  • The different type of hardware interfaces are as follows:-

    1. Serial Port

      • It is also called RS-232 port. It is an interface that is used to connect devices that are capable of transferring data serially i.e. One bit at a time.
      • It has normally nine pu and are leveled with COM which means these ports are for communication purposes.

    2. Parallel Port

      • It is an interface that is used to connect the devices that are capable of transferring multiple bits at a time. It allows us to transfer & bit of time.
      • This port normally contains 25 pins and is used for faster speed devices such as printers, scanners, etc.

    3. USB port

      • The term USB stands for Universal Serial Bus and this bus is used to connect up to 127 peripherals to a micro computer through a single general port.
      • It doesn't need any type of adapter card and installation. It was designed to allow many peripherals to be connected using a single Standardized socket and improve the plug capability without rebooting the computer.

    4. Slots

      • It refers to the physical connectors or expansion port on a computer's motherboard.
      • It allows you to expand or enhance the capability of your computer by inserting various hardware components like RAM slots (for inserting RAM), PCI slot (Peripheral Component Interconnect) for Network card, AGP (Accelerated Graphic Port) slot for inserting Graphics cards etc.

    5. IDE (Integrated Drive Electronic) port

      • It is an electronic interface standard that defines the connection between a bus on a computer's motherboard and computers disk storage devices.

      • There are types of IDE:-

        1. PATA

          • It refers to Parallel Advanced Technology Attachment. Slower and older type of IDE port. These cables are long flat with a 40-pin connector on either side of the cables.
          • It supports data transfer rate up to 133 mbps.

        2. SATA

          • It refers to serial Advanced Technology Attachment. It is the newer and faster standard for connecting storage devices like hard drives to the computer motherboard.
          • SATA cables are long, thin and 7pin cables. It can transmit data twice faster than PATA.
          • SATA versions include SATA-I (1.5 GB/s), SATA-II (3GB/s), SATA-III (6GB/s).
          • It replaces PATA because it connects the storage device with more advanced technology like high to clock frequency, better error handling, hot swap ability etc.
